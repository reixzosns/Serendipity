Chapter 1 - Ordinary Days

It was just like any other day. I woke up at 4 AM, long before the sun had even begun to rise. The room was still dark, and the only light came from the faint glow of my digital clock sitting on the desk beside my bed. The red numbers stared back at me, reminding me that another day had begun.

I sat up slowly and rubbed my eyes. For a moment, I considered lying back down and stealing a few more minutes of sleep. Nobody would know, right? But I knew better. If I went back to sleep, I'd probably wake up late and spend the entire morning rushing around.

With a sigh, I got out of bed.

The cold floor immediately greeted my feet. I stretched my arms above my head and yawned before making my way to the bathroom. Looking at myself in the mirror, I couldn't help but laugh a little.

"Man, I look terrible."

My hair looked like it had survived a hurricane. After washing my face and fixing my hair, I felt slightly more alive. Not much, but enough to function.

The house was silent. My parents were still asleep, and so was my younger sister. The only sounds were the occasional hum of the refrigerator and the distant barking of a dog somewhere outside.

I quietly headed to the kitchen and prepared breakfast. Nothing fancy. Just some bread and eggs. Cooking wasn't exactly my favorite thing to do, but after years of waking up early, I had gotten used to taking care of myself in the mornings.

As I ate, I scrolled through my phone

The usual stuff.

A few messages from classmates. Random videos. Homework reminders.

Nothing out of the ordinary.

Honestly, most days felt the same. School. Homework. Sleep. Repeat.

I wasn't the most popular student, but I wasn't invisible either. I had a few close friends, decent grades, and a normal life. There was nothing particularly exciting about me.

I was just Alex Williams. A grade 10 student trying to survive another school year.

By 5 AM, I had finished eating and started getting ready for school. I packed my notebooks, checked my assignments twice, and made sure I hadn't forgotten anything important.

I learned that lesson the hard way after showing up to school without a project once.

That was a nightmare.

Once everything was ready, I slung my backpack over my shoulder and stepped outside. The morning air was cool and refreshing.

The streets were mostly empty. Streetlights still illuminated the sidewalks, casting long shadows across the pavement. A few people were already awake, preparing their stores or heading to work.

I always liked this part of the day, everything felt so peaceful. The city hadn't fully woken up yet. No loud traffic. No crowded hallways. No teachers assigning work. Just silence.

As I walked toward the bus stop, I put on my headphones and played some music. The familiar melodies filled my ears while I watched the sky slowly change colors. Dark blue became purple. Purple became orange. And before I knew it, the sun was beginning to rise. The bus arrived shortly afterward. I climbed aboard and found my usual seat near the back.

Students gradually filled the bus at each stop. Some were chatting loudly. Others looked half asleep. I was somewhere in between. The ride to school took around thirty minutes, which gave me plenty of time to mentally prepare myself for the day ahead. Or at least try to.

When the school finally came into view, I felt the same thing I always felt, A mixture of boredom and anticipation.

Boredom because it was school. Anticipation because no matter how ordinary life seemed, you never really knew what could happen next.

At the time, I thought this day would be no different from all the others. At the time, I thought this day would be no different from all the others. Just another normal day. Just another ordinary chapter in my life.

I had no idea that before the day was over, everything was about to change.

And it would all begin with a single person.


Chapter 2 - The Transfer Student


The first few hours of school went exactly as I expected.

Boring.

Painfully boring.

The moment I entered the classroom, I was greeted by the usual chaos. Students were talking loudly, some were rushing to copy homework they had forgotten to do, and a few were simply staring at their phones before class started.

It was the same scene every day. Nothing ever changed.

I dropped my backpack beside my desk and sat down. A few seconds later, someone slapped a hand on my shoulder.

I didn't even need to turn around to know who it was.

Ethan: Alex!

Alex: Morning.

Ethan: You look dead.

Alex: Yeah, I woke up at 4 AM.

Ethan: You always wake up at four.

Alex: Exactly.

Ethan: That's not something to be proud of.

I shrugged.

Ethan laughed before pulling up a chair beside my desk.

We've been friends since seventh grade. Somehow, despite our completely different personalities, we got along surprisingly well.

Ethan was energetic. Too energetic.

Meanwhile, I preferred peace and quiet.

Yet somehow, it worked.

Ethan: So, ready for another exciting day of education?

Alex: I'd rather get hit by a bus.

Ethan: Fair.

The first bell rang before either of us could continue.

Everyone returned to their seats as our teacher entered the room. The morning classes dragged on forever.

Math felt like it lasted three hours.

Science wasn't much better.

History somehow managed to make thirty minutes feel like an entire lifetime.

By lunchtime, I felt exhausted.

Not physically. Mentally.

The cafeteria was packed as usual.

Students filled nearly every table while conversations echoed throughout the room.

After searching for a few minutes, Ethan and I managed to find an empty table near one of the windows. I opened my lunch while Ethan immediately began complaining.

Ethan: Why does every school lunch period feel like a battlefield?

Alex: Because there are seven hundred hungry teenagers in one room.

Ethan: Good point.

For a while, everything seemed normal.

Then the speakers suddenly crackled to life, and the entire cafeteria slowly became quieter.

Everyone turned their attention toward the front. The principal stood beside a microphone.

Principal: Good afternoon, students. We have an announcement regarding a new transfer student who will be joining Grade 10 starting today.

Instantly, whispers spread throughout the cafeteria.

A transfer student?

This late into the school year?

Who is it?

That was unusual.

Ethan: Think it's a guy or a girl?

Alex: How would I know?

Ethan: Maybe they're secretly a spy from another school.

Alex: Or Maybe they're secretly normal.

Ethan: You always ruin the fun.

I smirked.

Honestly, I didn't think much about the announcement.

People transferred schools all the time.

It wasn't a big deal.

Eventually lunch ended, and everyone returned to their classrooms.

As I walked inside, I immediately noticed something different.

Our teacher wasn't alone. Standing beside her was a girl I'd never seen before.

The entire classroom noticed.

Conversations stopped almost immediately.

Even the loudest students suddenly became interested.

The teacher smiled.

Teacher: Class, I'd like you all to welcome our newest student.

The girl stepped forward.

She looked surprisingly calm considering she was standing in front of a room full of strangers. Her dark hair rested neatly on her shoulders, and her eyes quietly scanned the classroom.

Then she smiled.

Emily: Hello, everyone. My name is Emily Parker. My family recently moved here, so I'll be studying with you from now on. I hope we can get along.

Simple. Polite. Nothing unusual.

Yet somehow, everyone seemed completely focused on her.

The teacher glanced around the room.

Teacher: Emily, you can sit over there.

She pointed toward an empty seat, my empty neighboring seat.

Of course.

I watched as Emily walked across the classroom.

Every pair of eyes seemed to follow her.

When she finally reached my desk, she stopped.

Emily: Is this seat taken?

Alex: For some reason, my brain completely stopped working. I stared at her for a second longer than I should have.

Alex: Uh... no.

Great.

Brilliant response.

Absolutely incredible communication skills.

Emily smiled.

Emily: Thanks.

Then she sat down. That was it. Five seconds. A single conversation.

Nothing important.

At least that's what I told myself.

The lesson continued normally afterward.

Or at least, it should have.

Yet every now and then, I found my attention drifting toward the empty notebook Emily was organizing on her desk.

She seemed focused, quiet, and focused.

Different from most of the students in our class.

When the final bell eventually rang, everyone immediately started packing their things, Including me.

As I grabbed my backpack, a notebook suddenly slipped from Emily's desk and landed on the floor.

Without thinking, I picked it up.

Alex: Here.

Emily: Oh, thank you.

Alex: No problem.

Emily: You're Alex, right?

I blinked.

Alex: Yeah.

Emily: Nice to meet you, Alex.

Then she smiled again.

A simple smile, nothing more.

Yet for some reason, it stayed in my mind long after she walked away.

As I headed toward the school gates that afternoon, Ethan suddenly appeared beside me.

Ethan: So.

Alex: So?

Ethan: The transfer student knows your name.

Alex: We sit next to each other.

Ethan: Still counts.

Alex: You're impossible.

Ethan: And you're blushing.

Alex: I am not.

Ethan: Sure, sure.

Alex: Can we just go home?

I rolled my eyes and continued walking.

But despite my protests, I couldn't stop thinking about that brief interaction.

At the time, it felt insignificant.

Just a normal conversation.

Just another ordinary moment in an ordinary day. I had no idea it would become the beginning of something much bigger.

Something that would change my life in ways I couldn't even imagine.


Chapter 3 - A New Friend


The next morning started exactly the same way as every other morning.

My alarm went off at 4:00 AM.

I groaned, reached over to my desk, and turned it off before it could wake up the rest of the house. For a few seconds, I stared at the ceiling, wondering why I had ever gotten into the habit of waking up this early. Most people my age were probably still deep asleep, dreaming about whatever teenagers dreamed about.

Meanwhile, I was already preparing for another school day.

After getting ready and eating breakfast, I left the house just as the first signs of sunrise began to appear on the horizon. The streets were quiet, and the cool morning air felt refreshing against my skin. Normally, I enjoyed these peaceful walks.

Today, however, my mind kept drifting back to yesterday.

More specifically, to Emily Parker.

I didn't know why.

It wasn't like we had some deep conversation. We had exchanged maybe three sentences in total. She had asked if the seat beside me was taken. I had responded like someone whose brain had completely stopped functioning. Then she thanked me for picking up her notebook.

That was it.

Yet somehow, I kept replaying those moments in my head. Maybe it was because she was new. Maybe it was because she seemed different from everyone else. Or maybe Ethan's endless teasing had gotten into my head.

Whatever the reason was, I found myself thinking about her more than I wanted to admit.

By the time I arrived at school, the campus was already beginning to fill with students. Groups of friends stood around talking while others rushed toward their classrooms.

As I entered our room, I immediately noticed something unusual.

Emily was already there.

In fact, she was the only student in the room.

She sat quietly at her desk, reading a book while sunlight streamed through the classroom windows.

For a moment, I just stood there.

I wasn't sure whether I should say something.

Eventually, I decided that standing in the doorway like an idiot was probably worse.

Alex: Morning.

Emily looked up from her book.

For a second, she seemed surprised.

Then she smiled.

Emily: Good morning.

I sat down at my desk and began unpacking my things. The silence wasn't awkward exactly.

It was just... quiet.

A few moments passed before Emily closed her book.

Emily: You're here pretty early.

Alex: I could say the same thing.

Emily: Fair enough.

There was a brief pause.

Emily: Do you always arrive this early?

Alex: Pretty much.

Emily: Why?

Alex: Honestly? I don't know anymore. I've been doing it for so long that it feels weird not to.

Emily laughed softly.

It wasn't loud or dramatic.

Just genuine.

Emily: That's probably the most honest answer I've heard all week.

For some reason, hearing her laugh made me smile a little.

Soon, more students began entering the classroom, including Ethan.

The moment he spotted me sitting beside Emily, his eyes widened.

That was never a good sign.

Ethan: Well, well, well.

I immediately sighed.

Alex: Don't.

Ethan: Don't what?

Alex: Whatever you're about to do.

Ethan: I haven't done anything.

Alex: Yet.

Ethan grinned before turning toward Emily.

Ethan: Hi, I'm Ethan. Alex's best friend and the person responsible for making sure he doesn't become a complete robot.

Emily: Nice to meet you.

Ethan: Trust me, you're going to need all the luck you can get sitting next to him.

Alex: Ethan.

Ethan: What? I'm helping.

Emily laughed again.

Unfortunately, Ethan seemed encouraged by this.

The rest of the morning passed relatively quickly. During class, I noticed that Emily was surprisingly smart. Whenever teachers asked questions, she answered confidently without sounding arrogant. She paid attention, took notes, and somehow managed to stay awake through history class.

I honestly found that impressive.

By lunchtime, the three of us ended up walking toward the cafeteria together.

Most of the conversation was carried by Ethan. Which wasn't surprising.

If silence was my specialty, talking was Ethan's.

Ethan: So where'd you move from?

Emily: A city about three hours away.

Ethan: Big adjustment?

Emily: Kind of. I'm still getting used to everything.

Ethan: Makes sense.

She glanced around the cafeteria.

Emily: This school is a lot bigger than my old one.

Alex: You'll get used to it.

Emily: Hopefully.

After lunch, we headed toward our next class. Unfortunately, the school building was large enough that getting lost wasn't exactly difficult.

And that became obvious about an hour later. The final bell rang, and students flooded into the hallways.

I was heading toward the front gate when I heard someone calling my name.

Emily: Alex!

I turned around.

She was standing near the intersection of two hallways, holding a folded map of the campus.

Alex: What's wrong?

Emily looked slightly embarrassed.

Emily: I think I'm lost.

I stared at her.

Then at the map.

Then back at her.

Alex: You got lost using a map?

Emily: In my defense, it's a very confusing map.

I couldn't help laughing.

Emily: Hey, You're supposed to be helping me.

Alex: Sorry.

For the next fifteen minutes, I helped Emily find the building she was looking for. Along the way, we ended up talking more than we ever had before.

We talked about classes.

Favorite subjects.

Hobbies.

Music.

Movies.

Books.

And even our families.

The conversation flowed surprisingly naturally.

Usually, I wasn't great at talking to people I had just met.

Yet with Emily, it felt easy.

Like I didn't have to constantly think about what to say next.

At one point, we passed by the school's garden area.

Emily slowed down slightly.

Emily: This place is nice.

Alex: Most students don't even notice it.

Emily: Really?

Alex: They're usually too busy rushing somewhere.

Emily looked around at the flowers swaying gently in the afternoon breeze.

Emily: That's kind of sad.

For a moment, neither of us spoke.

The campus felt strangely peaceful.

Almost frozen in time.

Then Emily smiled.

Emily: Thanks for helping me.

Alex: No problem.

Emily: You know, when I first walked into class yesterday, I thought everyone would be difficult to talk to.

Alex: Why?

Emily: New school. New people. New everything.

I nodded, that made sense.

Emily: But I'm glad I was wrong.

Something about the way she said it felt sincere.
Not forced.
Not polite.
Just genuine.

And for some reason, that mattered

Eventually, we reached her destination.

Emily: Looks like I won't be getting lost anymore.

Alex: Give it a week.

Emily: Wow. Such confidence.

Alex: Just being realistic.

Emily laughed.

Emily: See you tomorrow, Alex.

Alex: See you.

I watched as she walked away before finally turning toward home.

As I made my way through the school gates, I felt strangely lighter than usual. The day hadn't been extraordinary. Nothing dramatic had happened. No major events. No major events. No life-changing moments.

Just conversations.

Simple conversations.

Yet somehow, they were enough to make an ordinary day feel different.

At the time, I thought I was simply making a new friend.

Looking back now, I realize that was the day everything truly began.

The day Emily Parker stopped being "the transfer student."

And started becoming someone important to me.


Chapter 4 - Different Beginnings


The days after that started to feel… different.

Not in a dramatic, life-changing way. Nothing obvious happened that would make someone stop and say, “Yeah, everything changed right there.” It was more subtle than that. The kind of change you only notice when you look back later and realize things weren’t the same anymore.

Emily was still just Emily.

And I was still just Alex.

But somehow, the space between us at school didn’t feel as empty as it used to.

That morning, I arrived at school at my usual time. The sky was still soft and pale, like the sun hadn’t fully decided whether it wanted to rise yet. The hallways were quiet when I entered the classroom, and for once, I wasn’t the first one there.

Emily was already sitting at her desk again.
Same spot. Same posture. Same calm expression.

But this time, she wasn’t reading.

She was just staring at the window, watching the light move across the school grounds.

For a second, I hesitated at the door.

It was strange how something so small—just seeing someone sitting there before everyone else—could feel… familiar already.

Alex: Morning.

She turned slightly, like she had been expecting the voice.

Emily: Good morning.

I walked to my seat and sat down. Normally, silence between two people I barely knew would feel awkward. Something I would rush to fill with noise or distraction.

But with Emily, it didn’t feel like that.
It felt… calm.

Then she spoke again.

Emily: You’re always early.

I glanced at her briefly.

Alex: You’re always earlier.

That earned a small smile from her.

Not a big one. Not dramatic. Just something subtle, like she found the situation mildly funny.

Emily: I guess we’re both consistent, then.

Alex: Or both just bad at sleeping in.

That made her laugh quietly.

And again, I found myself thinking it was a sound I didn’t mind hearing.

The classroom slowly filled up as more students arrived. The usual noise returned—chairs scraping, backpacks dropping, loud greetings echoing across the room. Ethan arrived shortly after, of course, like he always did, making enough noise for three people.

He immediately noticed Emily and me sitting near each other.

His expression changed instantly.

That same expression.

The one that meant trouble.

Ethan: Oh no.

I didn’t even look up.

Alex: Don’t start.

Ethan: I didn’t say anything yet.

Alex: You were thinking it loud enough.

Emily looked between us, confused but amused.

Emily: Is something wrong?

Ethan: No. Nothing is wrong. Everything is actually very interesting.

Alex: Ethan.

He ignored me completely.

Ethan: I’m just observing. Documenting. Studying natural school behavior.

Emily: That sounds intense.

Ethan: It is. I take my job seriously.

Emily laughed again, and Ethan immediately looked satisfied like he had just accomplished something important in life.

\

I sighed and leaned back in my chair.

There was no winning with him.

Classes started not long after, and the day blended into its usual rhythm. Teachers talked, students copied notes, time moved slowly in that way it always did when you wanted it to go faster.

But even during the boring parts, I noticed small things.

Emily didn’t just listen in class, she actually paid attention.

When the teacher asked questions, she answered without hesitation. Not in a show-off way, but in a simple, straightforward way, like she wasn’t afraid of being wrong.

During group work, she didn’t try to take over or stay silent either. She just… participated normally. Like she had always been part of the class.

That probably shouldn’t have stood out to me.

But it did.

Because most people didn’t feel that natural to be around.

By lunch, the three of us ended up together again without really planning it. It just… happened.

We sat in a quieter corner of the cafeteria this time. Ethan, of course, still managed to make it feel less quiet than it actually was.

He was talking about something ridiculous—some story from his childhood that didn’t seem to have a point—but Emily was listening anyway, actually paying attention like it mattered.

I mostly stayed quiet, eating and watching the flow of conversation.

At some point, Ethan leaned back in his chair and pointed between us.

Ethan: You two are weirdly comfortable already.

I almost choked on my drink.

Alex: We’re literally just talking.

Ethan: Exactly.

Emily tilted her head slightly.

Emily: Is that a bad thing?

Ethan thought about it for half a second.

Ethan: No. It’s just unusual for Alex.

I shot him a look.

Alex: I’m sitting right here.

Ethan: I know.

Emily smiled faintly again, like she was trying to understand something she didn’t fully have context for yet.

After lunch, we split for classes again. The rest of the afternoon passed normally, but my mind didn’t fully stay in it. It kept drifting back to small moments. Conversations that didn’t feel important at the time but somehow stayed anyway.

And then came the end of the day.

I was leaving the building when I saw her again.

Emily was standing near the school entrance, holding her bag strap loosely, looking around like she was thinking about something but not saying it out loud.

When she noticed me, she lifted her hand slightly.

Emily: Alex.

I slowed down.

Alex: Yeah?

There was a short pause before she spoke again.

Emily: Do you ever feel like… some days are different, but you don’t know why?

That question caught me off guard.

I didn’t answer immediately.

I looked at her for a moment, trying to figure out what exactly she meant.

Alex: I think… yeah.

Another pause.

The air around us felt quieter than usual, even though the school was still full of noise behind us.

Emily nodded slowly, like that answer made sense to her.

Emily: Me too.

Then she smiled again, but this time it looked a little more distant than before.

Emily: See you tomorrow, Alex.

Alex: Yeah. See you.

She walked away after that.

And for some reason, I stood there a little longer than I needed to.

Like my brain was trying to hold onto something I didn’t fully understand yet.

That night, I couldn’t stop thinking about her question.

Some days are different, but you don’t know why.

I didn’t know it then.

But that was exactly what was happening.

And I was already in the middle of it.


Chapter 5 - Familiar


Humans are strange.

We spend most of our lives surrounded by people. We pass hundreds of strangers every week. We sit beside classmates, walk past neighbors, and stand among crowds without giving much thought to any of them.

Yet somehow, every once in a while, someone manages to stand out.

Not because they're extraordinary.

Not because they're loud.

Not because they're trying to get your attention.

They just do.

And before you realize it, seeing them becomes part of your routine.

That was what was happening with Emily.

Not that I wanted to admit it.

The next few days passed quietly.

School continued as usual. Teachers assigned homework. Students complained about homework. Ethan somehow found new reasons to complain about homework.

Everything felt normal.

And yet, whenever I looked back on those days later, Emily was somehow present in every memory.

Sometimes it was something small.

A conversation before class.

A joke during lunch.

A random comment while walking through the hallways.

Nothing important on its own.

But together, those moments started adding up.

One morning, I arrived at school to find Emily reading at her desk again.

At this point, I wasn't even surprised anymore.

Alex: Let me guess. You got here before me again.

Emily looked up from her book.

Emily: By exactly three minutes.

Alex: You've been keeping track?

Emily: Maybe.

Alex: That's slightly concerning.

Emily: Says the person who wakes up at four in the morning.

Alex: Fair.

She smiled and returned to her book.

And somehow, that tiny interaction improved my mood.

It was weird. The kind of weird I didn't fully understand.

More students gradually entered the classroom, and eventually Ethan arrived.

The moment he noticed us talking, he looked far too pleased.

Ethan: Ah yes.

I immediately knew where this was going.

Alex: No.

Ethan: Ah yes.

Alex: Ethan.

Ethan: I leave you two alone for five minutes and suddenly you're discussing books.

Emily: We exchanged three sentences.

Ethan: That's how it starts.

Alex: What starts?

Ethan: History.

Emily laughed.

Traitor.

For the rest of the morning, Ethan continued finding ways to annoy me whenever possible.

Unfortunately, Emily seemed to find his nonsense entertaining.

Which only encouraged him.

By lunchtime, the three of us had unofficially developed a routine.

Nobody ever suggested sitting together. Nobody planned it.

It simply happened.
Every day.
The same table.
The same conversations.
The same familiar faces.

It felt natural.

Dangerously natural.

At one point during lunch, Ethan suddenly pointed at Emily.

Ethan: Serious question.

Emily: That's already worrying.

Ethan: What's your favorite movie?

Emily blinked.

Emily: That's your serious question?

Ethan: Absolutely.

She thought for a moment.

Emily: Probably A Silent Voice.

Ethan: Solid answer.

He turned toward me.

Ethan: Alex?

Alex: I don't know.

Ethan: How do you not know your favorite movie?

Alex: Because I don't spend my free time ranking films.

Ethan: You're impossible.

Emily: What's yours?

Ethan immediately sat up straighter.

That was a mistake.

The next five minutes were spent listening to him passionately explain why his favorite action movie was "criminally underrated."

Emily listened politely.

I questioned my life choices.

Eventually, classes resumed.

The day continued normally until our English teacher announced a new project.

The entire class groaned.

Teacher: You'll be working in pairs.

The groaning somehow became louder.

Teacher: I'll be assigning the groups myself.

Now everyone looked concerned.

Nobody trusted teacher-assigned groups.

Nobody.

The teacher began reading names. One pair after another.

Students slowly discovered their fates.

Teacher: Alex Williams and Emily Parker.

I froze.

Across the room, Ethan nearly fell out of his chair.

Ethan: No way.

Alex: Be quiet.

Ethan: This is the greatest day of my life.

Alex: Please stop talking.

Emily looked equally amused.

Emily: He seems excited.

Alex: Unfortunately.

The teacher continued assigning groups while Ethan struggled to contain himself.

Keyword: struggled.

After class ended, Emily walked over to my desk.

Emily: Looks like we're partners.

Alex: Looks like it.

Emily: Are you free after school tomorrow?

For a brief moment, my brain stopped functioning again.

Not as badly as the first time.

But close.

Alex: Yeah.

Emily: Great.

Alex: Great.

There was a pause.

Emily: You're doing that thing again.

Alex: What thing?

Emily: Looking like your brain crashed.

I stared at her.

She laughed.

And just like that, the awkwardness disappeared.

The next afternoon, we met in the school library.

The library was surprisingly quiet.

Sunlight filtered through the large windows, illuminating rows of bookshelves stretching across the room.

Emily arrived carrying several notebooks.

I arrived carrying exactly one.

She noticed immediately.

Emily: You came prepared.

Alex: Absolutely.

Emily: You brought one notebook.

Alex: One very important notebook.

Emily: Incredible.

For the next hour, we worked on our project.

Or at least attempted to.

The problem wasn't the assignment.

The problem was that we kept getting distracted.

One conversation led to another.

Which led to another.

And another.

Before long, we had somehow gone from discussing English literature to discussing favorite songs.

Then favorite foods.

Then childhood memories.

Then embarrassing stories.

Emily: Wait.

She looked horrified.

Emily: You actually tried cutting your own hair?

Alex: I was eight.

Emily: That explains nothing.

Alex: It seemed like a good idea.

Emily: Did it?

Alex: At the time.

Emily laughed so hard she had to cover her face.

Honestly?

I couldn't even be embarrassed.

Watching her laugh made the story worth telling.

Hours passed faster than either of us expected.

Eventually, the library began emptying.

Students headed home.

The afternoon sunlight slowly turned golden.

Neither of us noticed until the librarian walked past.

Librarian: Closing in fifteen minutes.

Emily looked toward the windows.

Emily: Wait.

Alex: What?

Emily: We've been here for three hours.

I checked my phone.

She was right.

Three hours.

Neither of us had realized.

For some reason, that fact lingered in my mind.

Three hours should have felt long.

Instead, it felt like thirty minutes.

As we packed our things, Emily suddenly became quieter.

Not sad.

Just thoughtful.

We left the library together and started walking toward the front gates.

The campus was nearly empty now.

The setting sun painted everything in shades of gold and orange.

For a while, neither of us spoke. Then Emily broke the silence.

Emily: Can I ask you something?

Alex: Sure.

She looked ahead, not at me.

Ahead.

As if she were carefully choosing her words.

Emily: Do you ever think about how weird life is?

Alex: That's a very broad question.

Emily: I know.

I waited.

Emily: A month ago, I didn't know this school existed.

A small breeze passed between us.

Emily: I didn't know any of the people here.

I nodded.

Emily: And now... I don't know.

She laughed softly.

Emily: It just feels strange.

For a moment, I understood exactly what she meant.

Life changed quietly.
Not all at once.
Not dramatically.

Sometimes everything shifted without announcing itself.

Sometimes people entered your life unexpectedly.

And before you knew it, you couldn't imagine your routine without them.

Alex: Yeah.

Emily looked at me.

Alex: I think I get it.

For a second, neither of us spoke.

Then she smiled.

Not the polite smile she gave teachers.

Not the amused smile she gave Ethan.

A different one.

Warmer.

More genuine.

And somehow, seeing it made my chest feel oddly light.

By the time we reached the school gates, the sun was beginning to disappear beyond the horizon.

Emily: Thanks for helping with the project.

Alex: We actually finished surprisingly little.

Emily: Details.

Alex: Fair.

She adjusted her bag.

Emily: See you tomorrow?

The question was simple.

Casual.

Normal.

Yet for some reason, I found myself smiling.

Alex: Yeah.

Emily: Good.

Then she waved and started walking away.

I stood there for a few seconds longer than necessary.
